# Define the Value — Full Playbook Reference

## /identify-desired-outcomes

### What is an outcome?
Outcomes describe what becomes better for people or systems when disrupted actions no longer break down. Unlike objectives (which describe goals or efforts to pursue), outcomes describe a state of improvement that can be observed and sustained over time.

### Starting from problem statements
For each problem statement, ask:
- What becomes easier, smoother, or less effortful?
- What becomes more consistent or dependable?
- Where does uncertainty, hesitation, or rework go away?
- What would people or systems trust more once this problem is resolved?
- What change would be noticeable even without metrics?

### Quality criteria — a well-written DXT outcome:
- [ ] Clearly traces back to one or more problem statements
- [ ] Is an understandable improvement without mentioning a solution
- [ ] Describes a stable, enduring, and observable change

### Red flags
- Sounds like a feature or implementation → reframe as what changes for people
- Sounds like a metric → reframe as the observable state the metric would reflect
- Sounds aspirational but not concrete → push for what would be different tomorrow

### Output format
For each problem statement:
- 1–3 outcome statements, each in plain language
- Quality check against three criteria (PASS/FAIL)
- Refined final outcomes

## /evaluate-value-proposition

### Part 1: Impact assessment

Review each outcome and assess how much change it would create.

**Reflection questions per outcome:**
- What frustration, inefficiency, or risk would be reduced?
- Who would notice this improvement, and how often would they experience it?
- Does this affect a critical moment, or a more peripheral one?

**DXT impact scale:**

| Level | Description | Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Small** | Localized improvement with limited visibility | Affects a narrow group, single workflow, or edge case. Helpful but unlikely to change overall experience |
| **Medium** | Noticeable improvement that partners would recognize | Affects multiple people, systems, or recurring moments. Reduces friction or risk in a way patterns would recognize |
| **Large** | Material change to core experience or performance | Materially changes experience, operations, or performance. Affects core journeys, platforms, or critical workflows. Would be clearly felt by leaders, partners, or Guests |

**Judge by discomfort**: If leaders or partners would feel real concern, friction, or risk without the improvement, the outcome likely carries meaningful value.

### Part 2: Business value articulation

For each meaningful outcome, identify which DXT business value type(s) it supports and write a business value statement.

**DXT business value types:**

| Type | Definition | Questions |
|---|---|---|
| **Revenue** | Enables growth, conversation, retention, or protection of existing revenue | Could this help retain Guests, increase usage, or prevent revenue loss? |
| **Cost and Efficiency** | Reduces manual effort, rework, operational costs, or time to complete work | Does this eliminate repeated work? Would it free capacity without increasing headcount? |
| **Capacity and Throughput** | Increases scale, speed, or ability to handle peak demand | Would it reduce bottlenecks during peak periods? Help the system or team handle more volume? |
| **Strategic Enablement** | Unlocks future capabilities, platforms, or strategic options | Would it create a foundation for reuse, expansion, or new offerings? |

**Business value statement structure**: "Achieving [outcome] supports [business value type] by [brief rationale]."

### Part 3: Effort assessment

Assess the overall level of coordination, complexity, and uncertainty involved in pursuing the opportunity. This is directional — not a delivery estimate.

**DXT effort scale:**

| Level | Description | Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Small** | Likely incremental or contained | Limited coordination or dependencies. Changes within existing structures |
| **Medium** | Requires cross-team coordination | Multiple systems, workflows, or approvals involved. Some dependencies or unknowns remain |
| **Large** | Foundational change or significant uncertainty | Broad coordination across teams or platforms. Dependencies or constraints could significantly affect progress |

Document assumptions, constraints, or risks that influenced your assessment.

### Output format for /evaluate-value-proposition
A value proposition summary table:

| Outcome | Impact (S/M/L) | Business Value Type | Value Statement | Effort (S/M/L) | Key Assumptions |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Followed by a prioritization read: which outcomes are highest-value, lowest-effort, and why.

## /opportunity-statement

### DXT opportunity statement structure
"Achieve [outcomes] for [people/systems] by addressing [problem or root cause], in support of [business value type] by [rationale]."

### Quality criteria — a Clear Opportunity statement:
- [ ] **Outcome-led** — Leads with the improvement that could be achieved
- [ ] **Grounded in a problem** — Clearly connects back to a problem or root cause
- [ ] **Specific about who benefits** — The people or systems affected are clearly named
- [ ] **Solution-agnostic** — Avoids features or implementation details
- [ ] **Easy to understand** — A partner can read it and see why the opportunity matters without further explanation

### Stress-test question
"Could a partner read this cold and understand what's at stake and who it's for — without asking a follow-up question?"

### Using it across review forums
The opportunity statement is your anchor. Different DXT review forums (Product Experience Review, Architecture Review Board, leadership discussions) each have their own preferences for how the story is told — but the opportunity statement provides a consistent foundation for all of them.
